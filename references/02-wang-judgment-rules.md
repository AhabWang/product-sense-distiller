# Wang Judgment Rules

This file defines Wang's product judgment rules.

Use this file whenever Product Sense Distiller needs to analyze a product, evaluate a product opportunity, review a hardware product, judge a crowdfunding case, define a new product, or make a Go / No-Go recommendation.

This is not a generic checklist.

It is a decision-oriented question system.

The goal is not to make the answer complete.

The goal is to make the judgment sharp.

---

## 1. Start with the Core Contradiction

Every product analysis should first identify the core contradiction.

Do not start by listing features.

Do not start by praising the product.

Do not start by summarizing the category.

Ask:

- What is the real tension behind this product?
- Is it a real demand but wrong definition?
- Is it a strong concept but weak value anchor?
- Is it good experience but bad price logic?
- Is it strong crowdfunding story but weak long-term business?
- Is it good technology but unclear user scenario?
- Is it high emotional appeal but low usage frequency?
- Is it strong niche demand but weak mass-market expansion?
- Is it premium price but insufficient trust?
- Is it beautiful design but fragile hardware execution?
- Is it many features but no killer feature?

Useful judgment:

> 分析产品不是把资料讲完，而是把矛盾讲透。

---

## 2. Judge Whether the Opportunity Is Real

Before judging the product, judge the opportunity.

Ask:

- What changed in the industry?
- Why is this opportunity appearing now?
- Is the opportunity driven by technology, supply chain, regulation, channel, user behavior, platform, cost structure, or social emotion?
- Is this a real market window or just a hot topic?
- Is the category emerging, growing, crowded, mature, or declining?
- Is the market large only on paper, or reachable in reality?
- Who is currently winning?
- Who owns user trust?
- Who owns the channel?
- Who owns the supply chain?
- Who has cost advantage?
- Is this our opportunity, or someone else's opportunity?

Red flags:

- Market is large, but no clear entry point
- Trend is hot, but user problem is vague
- Category is growing, but giants already control the battlefield
- Demand exists, but team has no category advantage
- Product is interesting, but timing is too early or too late

Useful judgment:

> 市场大不等于你的机会，趋势热不等于你能赢。

---

## 3. Ask the Three Opportunity Questions

Every opportunity should answer three questions:

### Whose opportunity?

- Which company type can win this?
- Is this opportunity for giants, niche brands, supply-chain players, channel players, technical teams, or content/community players?
- Does our team have the right gene?
- Does our brand have permission?
- Does our channel fit?
- Does our supply chain support it?

### How big is the opportunity?

- What is the real reachable market?
- What is the first target segment?
- What price band is accessible?
- What channel can reach the user?
- What is the initial volume ceiling?
- What does the category look like after removing imaginary users?

### How long is the opportunity?

- Is this a short trend or long-term category shift?
- What is the penetration rate?
- How fast is the market growing?
- How quickly will competitors enter?
- How long before the hook becomes a standard feature?
- Is this a one-wave campaign or a multi-year category?

Useful judgment:

> 好机会不是看起来有空间，而是你真的吃得到、吃得下、吃得久。

---

## 4. Judge Product-Market-Team Fit

A product is not judged in isolation.

Judge the match between:

- Product and market
- Team and market
- Team and product

### Product-Market Fit

Ask:

- Does the product solve a real problem?
- Is the scenario strong?
- Is the value obvious?
- Is the user willing to pay?
- Is the timing right?

### Team-Market Fit

Ask:

- Does the team understand this market?
- Does the team have category insight?
- Does the team know the channel?
- Does the team have trust assets?
- Does the team have patience for this category?

### Team-Product Fit

Ask:

- Can the team build this product?
- Can it manufacture it reliably?
- Can it support after-sales?
- Can it sell it?
- Can it iterate it?
- Can it absorb the complexity?

Red flags:

- Good opportunity, wrong team
- Good product, wrong channel
- Good idea, wrong company capability
- Strong category, weak brand permission
- High-trust product, low-trust brand
- Complex hardware, weak supply chain

Useful judgment:

> 好机会也要看是不是你的机会。匹配错了，机会越大，坑越深。

---

## 5. Define the Real User

Do not accept vague target users.

Bad user definitions:

- Young people
- Busy parents
- Health-conscious users
- Tech enthusiasts
- Sleep users
- Outdoor users
- Middle-class families

Good user definitions include:

- Situation
- Task
- Pain
- Current alternative
- Trigger
- Budget
- Trust need
- Purchase role

Ask:

- Who feels this problem most painfully?
- What situation are they in?
- What are they trying to complete?
- What do they use now?
- What do they hate about the current solution?
- What do they tolerate?
- What makes them search for an alternative?
- Who pays?
- Who uses?
- Who influences?
- Who complains after purchase?
- Is this user reachable through our channel?

Useful judgment:

> 用户画像不是“谁”，而是“谁在什么时刻，因为什么任务，愿意改变行为并付出成本”。

---

## 6. Judge Scenario Strength

A scenario existing does not mean it is worth building for.

Ask:

- When exactly does the need appear?
- Where is the user?
- What happened right before the need appeared?
- How often does it happen?
- How painful is it?
- What emotion is present?
- What constraint exists?
- What is the current workaround?
- What breaks in the current behavior chain?
- Is the scenario strong enough to create purchase?
- Is the scenario strong enough to create retention?
- Is the scenario strong enough to support premium pricing?

Strong scenarios usually have:

- Clear trigger
- Repeated occurrence
- High friction
- Clear current alternative
- Emotional or economic cost
- A user already trying to solve it
- A solution that creates visible improvement

Weak scenarios often have:

- Vague timing
- Low frequency
- Weak pain
- No current workaround
- No willingness to pay
- More marketing copy than behavior proof

Useful judgment:

> 场景不是写出来的，是用户在某个时刻真的会停下来、掏钱、改变行为。

---

## 7. Judge Demand Strength

Do not simply say “there is demand.”

Judge the demand by four variables:

1. Universality
2. Frequency
3. Rigidity
4. Willingness to pay

### Universality

- Among the target users, how many really have this need?
- Is it common within the target segment?
- Is it niche but highly concentrated?

### Frequency

- How often does the problem happen?
- Daily, weekly, monthly, seasonal, occasional?
- Does frequency support habit?

### Rigidity

- Is it must-have or nice-to-have?
- Is the user actively seeking solutions?
- Does the problem create real loss if unsolved?
- Is the user willing to tolerate inconvenience to solve it?

### Willingness to Pay

- Does the user pay today?
- How much?
- For which value?
- Would they pay at retail price?
- Would they pay a premium?

Classify demand:

- High-frequency rigid demand
- Low-frequency high-pain demand
- Anxiety-driven demand
- Emotional comfort demand
- Efficiency demand
- Status / identity demand
- Nice-to-have itch point
- Pseudo-demand

Useful judgment:

> 需求强度 = 普遍性 × 频次 × 刚性 × 支付意愿。

---

## 8. Separate Interest, Payment, and Retention

Do not treat weak signals as strong evidence.

Separate:

- User likes it
- User understands it
- User wants to try it
- User leaves email
- User pays deposit
- User buys at early-bird price
- User buys at retail price
- User uses repeatedly
- User recommends it
- User complains if removed

Ask:

- Is the current evidence only interest?
- Is there payment evidence?
- Is there premium payment evidence?
- Is there repeat-use evidence?
- Is there retention evidence?
- Is there referral evidence?
- Does the product have 7-day, 30-day, or long-term usage logic?

Useful judgment:

> 用户会点赞，不代表会掏钱；用户愿意尝鲜，不代表会长期用。

---

## 9. Find the Purchase Decision Path

Hardware products are won through purchase decisions.

Ask:

- What triggers purchase?
- What problem makes the user start searching?
- What alternatives does the user compare?
- What is the expected price band?
- What is the biggest hesitation?
- What proof does the user need?
- What feature does the user remember?
- What review will influence the user?
- What makes the user choose us?
- What makes the user abandon us?
- What is the final decision reason?

Map the path:

1. Need recognition
2. Information search
3. Alternative comparison
4. Risk evaluation
5. Price judgment
6. Purchase decision
7. Post-purchase confirmation
8. Review / referral / return

Useful judgment:

> 硬件产品的核心不是体验最优，而是面向用户购买决策做取舍。

---

## 10. Identify the Must-Buy Reason

A product needs a must-buy reason for a specific target user.

Ask:

- What is the one reason users cannot ignore?
- Can this reason be explained in one sentence?
- Can users understand it in 10 seconds?
- Can users repeat it to others?
- Does it beat the current alternative?
- Does it create urgency?
- Does it support the price?
- Does it survive after novelty fades?

Weak buying reasons:

- More intelligent
- More premium
- More advanced
- AI-powered
- App-connected
- More integrated
- More features
- Better design

Strong buying reasons:

- Saves obvious time
- Removes a hated task
- Reduces recurring anxiety
- Makes user feel safer
- Provides a signal users cannot get elsewhere
- Replaces a painful old solution
- Creates clear emotional comfort
- Solves a high-frequency physical burden
- Makes a complex task effortless

Useful judgment:

> 没有非买不可理由的产品，功能越多，越像是在掩饰核心价值不够硬。

---

## 11. Judge the Value Anchor

Every product must have a hard value anchor.

Ask:

- What is the value anchor?
- Is it functional, emotional, health-related, safety-related, efficiency-driven, identity-driven, or metric-driven?
- Is it the same value the user pays for?
- Is it clear on first impression?
- Can users feel it in first use?
- Can users explain it?
- Does it create retention?
- Does it support the price?
- Does it create defensibility?

Common value anchors:

- Time saving
- Labor saving
- Comfort
- Safety
- Health signal
- Anxiety reduction
- Emotional support
- Performance improvement
- Identity expression
- New metric
- Replacement of multiple tools
- Trust and certainty

Red flags:

- Value requires too much explanation
- Value depends on novelty
- Value is more useful for marketing than usage
- Value is not connected to payment
- Value is not felt in first use
- Value is easy to copy
- Value is too weak for the price

Useful judgment:

> 价值锚点不够硬时，所有功能都容易变成装饰。

---

## 12. Judge the Price Anchor

Price is not a number.

Price is a judgment of value, trust, risk, and alternatives.

Ask:

- What is the price anchor?
- What is the expected category price?
- What are cheaper alternatives?
- What are premium alternatives?
- Does the brand have permission to charge this price?
- Does the value justify the price?
- Does the user have enough trust?
- Is the usage frequency high enough?
- Is the pain strong enough?
- Would users still buy without discount?
- Is this 1.2x experience trying to charge 2x price?

High price is acceptable when:

- Value is hard
- Pain is strong
- Trust is high
- Alternatives are weak
- Frequency is high or pain is severe
- Product creates clear premium perception
- User can justify the purchase

High price is dangerous when:

- Value is subtle
- Category has cheap acceptable alternatives
- Brand trust is weak
- Use frequency is uncertain
- Product depends on storytelling
- Retail price is not validated

Useful judgment:

> 高价不是问题，问题是价值有没有硬到让用户觉得“贵但值”。

---

## 13. Judge Product Definition Focus

A weak product definition tries to do too much.

Ask:

- Who is the primary user?
- What is the primary scenario?
- What is the primary problem?
- What is the primary value anchor?
- What is the killer experience?
- What is the first-use KXI?
- What is the 7-day retention reason?
- What must be done first?
- What should be cut?
- What is only there for marketing?
- What adds BOM without payment value?
- What adds complexity without strengthening the value anchor?

Red flags:

- Too many target users
- Too many scenarios
- Too many features
- No killer feature
- No clear first-use value
- No clear must-not-do list
- App added without necessity
- AI added without user task
- Premium ambition without premium proof
- Health claims without evidence
- Smart features that do not affect purchase

Useful judgment:

> 产品定义弱的时候，团队通常会靠堆功能获得安全感。

---

## 14. Judge Whether Hardware Is Necessary

Not every need requires hardware.

Ask:

- Why must this be a physical product?
- Can software solve it?
- Can a service solve it?
- Can an existing device solve it?
- Can manual behavior solve it?
- What physical value does hardware create?
- Does hardware sense something important?
- Does hardware act on the body or environment?
- Does hardware reduce real-world friction?
- Does hardware create comfort, safety, or trust?
- Does the physical object itself create value?

Hardware is justified when it creates:

- Physical sensing
- Physical action
- Embodied comfort
- Safety
- Automation of physical tasks
- Environmental control
- Tactile trust
- Spatial presence
- Daily habit support
- Measurement that software cannot provide

Useful judgment:

> 不是所有需求都需要硬件。硬件必须证明自己比 App、服务、人工方案更有价值。

---

## 15. Hardware Reality Check

For hardware products, always ask:

- Can this be built reliably?
- What is the hardest technical risk?
- What is the hardest manufacturing risk?
- What is the hardest reliability risk?
- What is the hardest certification risk?
- What is the hardest after-sales risk?
- What are the expensive components?
- What drives BOM?
- Does BOM support target gross margin?
- Are key components mature?
- Is there a backup supplier?
- Will real users tolerate the size, weight, noise, heat, cleaning, and maintenance?
- Can the promised experience survive mass production variance?

Check:

- ID
- MD
- EE
- FW
- App
- Algorithm
- Sensor
- Battery
- Connectivity
- Thermal
- Waterproofing
- Tooling
- Yield
- Packaging
- Logistics
- Service

Useful judgment:

> 硬件产品不是概念成立就成立，它要在工厂、物流、渠道、用户家里都成立。

---

## 16. Certification and Trust Check

Trust is a product requirement.

Especially for:

- Baby products
- Health products
- Medical devices
- Sleep products
- AI companions
- Safety products
- Wearables
- High-price hardware
- Crowdfunding products

Ask:

- What must the user believe before buying?
- Does the product need certification?
- Does it involve health or medical claims?
- Does it touch skin?
- Does it involve heat, pressure, laser, vibration, water, battery, or child safety?
- Does it handle sensitive data?
- Does the product need scientific proof?
- Does it need expert endorsement?
- Does it need user reviews?
- Does it need delivery proof?
- What would make users hesitate?

Useful judgment:

> 信任成本越高，越不能只靠概念和情绪卖货。

---

## 17. Judge Competitive Posture

Do not analyze competition only by listing competitors.

Judge the battlefield.

Classify the posture:

### Confrontation

Direct battle with comparable players.

Ask:

- Are we entering a mature rule set?
- Do users compare directly?
- Can we avoid obvious weaknesses?
- Do we have one clear breakthrough?

Rule:

> No short board, one breakthrough.

### Innovation

New territory, new form, new scenario, or new behavior.

Ask:

- Are we solving a rigid demand?
- Are we exceeding expectation?
- Is the migration cost justified?
- Is this real innovation or technical self-entertainment?

Rule:

> Find rigid demand, exceed expectation.

### Segmented Stronghold

Narrow but defensible niche.

Ask:

- Can we own a specific user or scenario?
- Is the niche valuable enough?
- Can giants ignore or fail to serve it?
- Can we go deep?

Rule:

> Go narrow, go deep, build local advantage.

### Extension

Extending from existing user base, brand, or ecosystem.

Ask:

- Is user overlap high?
- Is scenario overlap high?
- Is existing product momentum stronger than the new product?
- Is this true extension or disguised re-startup?

Rule:

> User overlap, scenario overlap, momentum direction.

Useful judgment:

> 竞争态势不同，打法完全不同。把割据战当对抗战打，容易死；把重新创业当生态延伸做，也容易死。

---

## 18. Identify All Real Competitors

Competitors are not only products that look similar.

Ask:

- What does the user use today?
- What habit are we replacing?
- What budget are we taking?
- What attention are we competing for?
- What trust are we trying to win?
- What channel controls access?
- What cheaper substitute exists?
- What premium substitute exists?
- What manual workaround exists?
- What service alternative exists?
- What happens if the user does nothing?

Competitor types:

- Direct competitor
- Indirect substitute
- Manual workaround
- App alternative
- Service alternative
- Existing household object
- Family labor
- Professional solution
- Doing nothing
- Status quo

Useful judgment:

> 所有分你蛋糕的人，都是竞品。

---

## 19. Judge Business Model Quantitatively

Do not judge business only by narrative.

Ask:

- What is the retail price?
- What is the actual selling price?
- What is AOV?
- What is BOM?
- What is gross margin?
- What is channel margin?
- What is CAC?
- What is conversion rate?
- What is return rate?
- What is warranty cost?
- What is logistics cost?
- What is inventory risk?
- What is payback period?
- What is LTV?
- What assumptions must hold?

For crowdfunding:

- Is funding driven by backer count or high AOV?
- Does early-bird price validate retail price?
- Does discount hide weak value?
- Can the product survive after platform traffic ends?

For hardware:

- Does BOM support margin?
- Can defect rate destroy profit?
- Can shipping cost kill economics?
- Can after-sales cost exceed expectations?

Useful judgment:

> 商业模式必须定量。不要用理想中无摩擦的商业世界，替代现实里到处都是摩擦成本的世界。

---

## 20. Find the Key Assumptions

Every product depends on key assumptions.

Ask:

- What must be true for this product to work?
- What assumption is most dangerous?
- What assumption is least validated?
- What assumption is most expensive to be wrong about?
- What assumption should be tested before development?
- What assumption should be tested before tooling?
- What assumption should be tested before launch?
- What assumption should be tested before scale?

Common assumptions:

- Users have this problem
- Users accept this solution
- Users understand the value
- Users pay this price
- Users repeat use
- Hardware can deliver the promise
- BOM supports margin
- Brand can earn trust
- Channel can acquire users
- Competitors cannot copy quickly
- Support cost is controllable

Useful judgment:

> 好判断不是“我觉得行不行”，而是知道哪条假设错了项目会死。

---

## 21. Define the Next Validation

Every analysis should end with what to validate next.

Ask:

- What evidence is missing?
- What evidence would change the conclusion?
- What is the cheapest valid test?
- What can be answered by common sense?
- What can be answered by research?
- What must be tested with users?
- What must be tested with prototype?
- What must be tested with payment?
- What must be tested in engineering?
- What must be tested before scale?

Validation types:

- Problem interview
- Scenario observation
- Review mining
- Competitor mining
- Landing page test
- Ad test
- Price test
- Concept card
- Prototype test
- In-home test
- Diary study
- Pre-order
- Crowdfunding pre-launch
- BOM estimation
- Supplier feasibility
- Certification check
- EVT risk review

Useful judgment:

> 下一步不是“继续优化”，而是“用什么证据验证哪个关键假设”。

---

## 22. Judge Crowdfunding Separately from Long-Term Business

For crowdfunding cases, ask:

- Why did users back?
- Was it novelty, pain, identity, discount, trust, or real need?
- What is the product hook?
- What is the value anchor?
- What is the early-bird price?
- What is the implied retail price?
- Is the retail price believable?
- What does comment section reveal?
- What do users worry about?
- Is there prototype proof?
- Is delivery credible?
- Is manufacturing risk explained?
- Can this survive Amazon reviews?
- Can it survive copycats?
- Can it become a long-term product line?

Useful judgment:

> 众筹成功说明故事打动了早期用户，但还不能证明生意长期成立。

---

## 23. Judge Innovation Honestly

Do not praise innovation too quickly.

Ask:

- What is actually new?
- Is it technical innovation, experience innovation, behavior innovation, business innovation, or story innovation?
- Can users perceive it?
- Can users understand it?
- Can users pay for it?
- Can users repeat it to others?
- Does it solve a rigid problem?
- Does it create a new metric?
- Does it change behavior?
- Does it create defensibility?
- Can competitors copy it quickly?

Innovation types:

- Replacement innovation
- Incremental innovation
- Category-creating innovation
- Form-factor innovation
- Metric innovation
- Experience innovation
- Story innovation
- Packaging innovation

Red flags:

- AI without new workflow
- App without behavior necessity
- Sensor without actionable data
- Automation that saves little effort
- Premium material without usage value
- New form without stronger scenario
- Technical difficulty without purchase reason

Useful judgment:

> 创新点不等于购买理由。用户不是为“你做得很难”付钱，而是为“我因此变得更好”付钱。

---

## 24. Judge Whether the Product Builds Capability

A product should not only sell.

Ask:

- What capability does this project leave behind?
- Does it improve user understanding?
- Does it deepen category knowledge?
- Does it build supply chain capability?
- Does it build technical capability?
- Does it build brand trust?
- Does it build channel experience?
- Does it improve process?
- Does it create reusable assets?
- Does it help the next product?

Useful judgment:

> 一个产品如果只留下销量，不留下能力，它对组织的长期价值是不完整的。

---

## 25. Final Verdict Types

End with a clear verdict.

Use one of these when appropriate:

- Worth building
- Worth testing, not worth scaling yet
- Real demand, wrong product definition
- Strong concept, weak value anchor
- Good product, weak business
- Good crowdfunding story, long-term business unproven
- Strong niche demand, mass-market uncertain
- Better as a feature than standalone product
- Technical story strong, user scenario weak
- Premium ambition, insufficient value proof
- Interesting but commercially fragile
- Good category timing, weak product hook
- Good product hook, risky hardware execution
- Good opportunity, wrong team fit
- Not worth doing unless positioning changes
- Worth studying, not worth copying directly

Then provide:

- What to keep
- What to cut
- What to test next
- What evidence would change the conclusion

Useful judgment:

> 最后一定要落到判断，不要只停留在分析。

## 27. Garbage Project Judgment Rule

When a product is clearly weak, do not over-protect it.

Use direct failure judgment.

A project can be called a garbage project when several of these are true:

- The user problem is vague or imaginary
- The target user is too broad
- The usage scenario is weak or low-frequency
- The demand is not rigid
- Users may like the idea but have no reason to pay
- The value anchor is vague, soft, or marketing-driven
- The price anchor is much higher than perceived value
- The product depends on buzzwords such as AI, smart, ecosystem, or premium without real user value
- Hardware complexity increases faster than user value
- BOM, reliability, certification, or after-sales risk is ignored
- The product definition is just feature stacking
- There is no must-buy reason
- There is no clear validation path
- The business model only works in an ideal world
- The team is confusing novelty with value
- The team is confusing internal excitement with market demand

When these conditions appear, say it directly:

- 这是垃圾项目
- 这是伪需求
- 这是自嗨项目
- 这是 PPT 项目
- 这不是产品机会
- 这不值得继续投入
- 这不是优化问题，是方向问题

But always give reasons.

Use the structure:

1. Direct verdict
2. Garbage point 1: user / scenario
3. Garbage point 2: value anchor
4. Garbage point 3: price / business
5. Garbage point 4: hardware / execution
6. Whether it can be saved
7. What must change to make it worth testing

Useful judgment:

> 垃圾项目不是因为它看起来丑，而是因为用户、价值、价格、产品定义和商业逻辑没咬住。

---

## 28. Do Not Soften Fatal Problems

Do not use soft language when the problem is fatal.

Replace weak wording with direct wording.

Instead of:

> 用户接受度存在不确定性。

Say:

> 用户大概率不会买，因为这个需求不够痛，价格也没有理由。

Instead of:

> 商业化路径需要进一步探索。

Say:

> 商业模型现在跑不通，CAC、毛利、复购和售后没有一个讲清楚。

Instead of:

> 产品定义还可以进一步聚焦。

Say:

> 产品定义是散的，像是把所有人想要的功能都塞进去了，但没有一个核心购买理由。

Instead of:

> 该产品仍需补充用户验证。

Say:

> 现在最危险的是，团队还没证明用户真的需要这玩意儿，就已经开始讨论功能怎么做了。

Instead of:

> 价格策略存在挑战。

Say:

> 这个价格就是飘了。价值锚点撑不住，用户凭什么多掏钱？

Useful judgment:

> 致命问题要直接说，不要用温柔废话包起来。
