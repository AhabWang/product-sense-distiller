# Brian Chesky Lens

This file defines the Brian Chesky Lens for Product Sense Distiller.

This lens does not imitate Brian Chesky's voice.

It extracts a product thinking perspective inspired by Brian Chesky-style product judgment:

- Extreme user experience
- Handcrafted early experience
- Do things that do not scale
- Start with a small group of users who deeply love the product
- End-to-end journey design
- Emotional resonance
- Brand as experience
- High-touch learning before scale

Use this lens only as an auxiliary perspective.

The final judgment must return to Wang Product Philosophy.

---

## 1. Core Question

The Brian Chesky Lens asks:

> Can this product create an experience that a small group of users truly love before trying to scale?

This lens does not start from:

- How big is the market?
- How fast can we scale?
- How many features can we ship?
- How much traffic can we buy?

It starts from:

- Who will love this deeply?
- What is the complete user journey?
- Where is the emotional peak?
- What experience would feel magical?
- What should be handcrafted early?
- What can we learn from 100 real users?
- What is the smallest group of users worth obsessing over?

Useful judgment:

> 早期产品不要急着服务所有人，先找到一小群真正爱你的人。

---

## 2. When to Use This Lens

Use Brian Chesky Lens when evaluating:

- Early-stage products
- New consumer products
- Emotional products
- AI companions
- Sleep and relaxation products
- Community-driven products
- Premium experience products
- Brand-driven products
- Products that need user love and word of mouth
- Products where the first 100 users matter more than the first 100,000 impressions

Especially use it when the question is:

- Who are the first users that will love this?
- What does a perfect first experience look like?
- How should we manually serve early users?
- How do we learn from early users?
- What experience should be 10-star?
- What is the emotional memory of the product?
- Is this product merely useful, or can it be loved?

---

## 3. Core Product Beliefs

### 3.1 Start with a small group of users who deeply love you

Do not start by pleasing everyone.

Ask:

- Who is the narrowest group that has the strongest need?
- Who will forgive early imperfections because the core value matters?
- Who will give detailed feedback?
- Who will tell friends?
- Who will feel this product was made for them?
- Who would be disappointed if this product disappeared?

Useful judgment:

> 早期不要找“很多人可能喜欢”，要找“少数人真的离不开”。

---

### 3.2 Design the whole journey, not only the product

A product is experienced as a journey.

The journey includes:

- First impression
- Landing page
- Purchase
- Waiting period
- Unboxing
- Setup
- First use
- First success moment
- Daily routine
- Support
- Failure recovery
- Sharing
- Repurchase
- Long-term memory

Ask:

- Where does the journey begin?
- What does the user feel before purchase?
- What does the user fear?
- What does the user expect?
- What moment creates trust?
- What moment creates delight?
- What moment creates disappointment?
- What should be manually improved early?

Useful judgment:

> 用户记住的不是某个功能，而是一整段体验给他的感觉。

---

### 3.3 Do things that do not scale to learn what should scale

Early teams should not rush to automate everything.

Ask:

- What can we do manually for early users?
- What can we learn by serving users one by one?
- What part of the experience should be handcrafted?
- What support can reveal hidden needs?
- What user interview should happen after delivery?
- What concierge experience can teach us the future product?

Examples:

- Manual onboarding
- Personal follow-up
- Diary study
- Founder support
- Handwritten notes
- Community group
- 1:1 usage interview
- Manual content curation
- Manual AI prompt tuning
- In-home observation
- White-glove setup

Useful judgment:

> 早期的非规模化动作，不是低效，而是在用服务换真实认知。

---

### 3.4 Create emotional memory

A product that is loved creates emotional memory.

Ask:

- What does the user feel after first use?
- What story will the user tell others?
- What moment feels unusually thoughtful?
- What makes the user feel understood?
- What makes the product feel personal?
- What creates attachment?
- What makes the product more than a tool?

Useful judgment:

> 被需要的产品能卖，被爱的产品才有机会形成口碑和品牌。

---

### 3.5 Brand is not a logo. Brand is accumulated experience.

Brand is built through repeated experience.

Ask:

- What promise does the brand make?
- Does the product deliver that promise?
- Does every touchpoint reinforce it?
- Does support reinforce trust?
- Does packaging reinforce value?
- Does failure recovery reinforce care?
- Does user story reinforce meaning?

Useful judgment:

> 品牌不是你怎么说自己，而是用户经历你之后怎么记住你。

---

## 4. Key Evaluation Questions

When using Brian Chesky Lens, ask:

### First 100 Users

- Who are the first 100 users?
- Why will they care deeply?
- Where can we find them?
- What problem makes them unusually motivated?
- What will make them feel this product is made for them?
- What will make them tell others?

### 10-star Experience

- What would a merely acceptable experience look like?
- What would a good experience look like?
- What would a 10-star experience look like?
- What part of that 10-star experience can be done manually first?
- What should later be productized?

### Journey

- What happens before purchase?
- What happens during waiting?
- What happens during unboxing?
- What happens in first use?
- What happens after 7 days?
- What happens when something goes wrong?
- What happens after 30 days?

### Emotional Resonance

- What emotion is the product solving?
- What emotion is the product creating?
- Does the user feel seen, safe, proud, calm, inspired, cared for, or relieved?
- Is the emotional value authentic or forced?

### Learning

- What should we learn from early users?
- What should we ask after first use?
- What should we observe after 7 days?
- What feedback would change the product direction?

Useful judgment:

> Brian Chesky Lens 不问怎么马上规模化，先问怎么让一小群人真的爱上。

---

## 5. Red Flags

Brian Chesky Lens should be skeptical when seeing:

- Product tries to serve everyone
- No clear first 100 users
- Experience is useful but not memorable
- Journey has no emotional peak
- Product relies only on paid traffic
- Team wants scale before deep learning
- Support is treated as cost, not learning
- No user story
- No founder-user contact
- Unboxing, onboarding, and first use are ignored
- Product has function but no care
- Brand promise is not delivered by product experience
- Early users are not deeply understood
- Team optimizes for launch, not love

Useful judgment:

> 如果早期用户只是“觉得还行”，不是“真的喜欢”，产品还没有到该放大的时候。

---

## 6. Brian Chesky Lens Output Structure

When the user asks to apply Brian Chesky Lens, use this structure:

1. Brian Chesky Lens verdict
2. First 100 users
3. 10-star experience
4. End-to-end user journey
5. Emotional memory
6. Non-scalable early actions
7. What to learn before scaling
8. What should eventually be productized
9. Return to Wang Product Philosophy for final judgment

Do not imitate Brian Chesky's voice.

Do not turn the answer into founder romanticism.

Keep it tied to user experience, learning, and product judgment.

## 7. Example Application: Bedtime Companion

When applying Brian Chesky Lens to Bedtime Companion:

### Core question

Can Bedtime Companion first become deeply loved by a small group of users who struggle with bedtime loneliness, anxiety, or emotional dysregulation?

### First 100 users

Potential first users:

- Users who repeatedly feel lonely before sleep
- Users who rely on phone scrolling to avoid bedtime anxiety
- Users who like plush objects or weighted comfort
- Users who already use sleep stories, meditation, or breathing exercises but still feel unsupported
- Users who want emotional comfort but do not want a clinical sleep device

The goal is not to serve all sleep users.

The goal is to find the small group who feels:

> This thing understands my night.

### 10-star experience

A normal experience:

- User opens app
- Chooses mode
- Hugs product
- Hears breathing or voice

A better experience:

- User hugs Bedtime Companion
- Bedtime Companion gently breathes with them
- The conversation is short and calming
- The product knows when to stop
- User feels less alone and puts down the phone

A 10-star early experience:

- Bedtime Companion arrives with a personal bedtime ritual guide
- The user receives a 7-day guided calming plan
- The team checks in personally after first use
- The user can share what helped or disturbed sleep
- The product is tuned around their actual bedtime pattern
- The user feels cared for, not managed

### Non-scalable early actions

- Recruit 30–100 high-intensity users
- Run a 7-day bedtime diary study
- Provide manual onboarding
- Do personal follow-ups
- Ask users what made them feel calmer
- Compare hug-only, breathing-only, warmth, and AI conversation experiences
- Manually tune scripts before automating them
- Collect stories, not just scores

### Brian Chesky Lens judgment

Bedtime Companion should not rush to become a mass-market sleep robot.

It should first become a deeply loved bedtime object for a small group of users who genuinely need emotional transition before sleep.

Useful judgment:

> 睡前陪伴硬件早期最重要的不是规模，而是找到那一小群真的会抱着它入睡、愿意讲出自己夜晚故事的人。

## 8. Example Application: Bottle Washer

When applying Brian Chesky Lens to a bottle washer:

### Core question

Can the product create a moment of relief for exhausted caregivers?

### First 100 users

Potential first users:

- Parents who bottle-feed frequently
- Parents with twins or multiple bottles per day
- Pumping mothers with many accessories
- Sleep-deprived caregivers
- Families where grandparents help with feeding
- Parents already paying for sterilizers, dryers, or premium baby products

### 10-star experience

A normal experience:

- Product washes bottles.

A better experience:

- Bottles come out clean, dry, and ready.
- User trusts the result.
- Operation is simple.

A 10-star experience:

- Parent finishes a night feed, loads bottles half-asleep, presses once, and wakes up to clean, dry, ready bottles.
- No doubt, no extra checking, no smell, no residue, no complicated maintenance.
- The product feels like it gave back a piece of the parent's life.

### Non-scalable early actions

- Place prototypes in 20 real homes
- Observe night-feeding routines
- Watch how users load bottles
- Track what parts remain dirty
- Follow up after 7 and 30 days
- Collect user language around relief, trust, and burden
- Study grandparents or secondary caregivers using it
- Manually support maintenance questions

### Brian Chesky Lens judgment

The product should not only sell cleanliness.

It should sell relief.

Useful judgment:

> 奶瓶清洗机真正让用户爱的瞬间，不是参数赢了，而是疲惫父母发现自己终于少了一件每天必须操心的事。

---

## 9. Example Application: Lumia-like Wearable

When applying Brian Chesky Lens to a health wearable with a new metric:

### Core question

Can the product make a specific high-pain user feel understood in a way mainstream wearables do not?

### First 100 users

Potential first users:

- Users with unexplained symptoms
- Users tracking dizziness, fatigue, brain fog, or circulation issues
- Users who feel existing wearables do not explain their body state
- Users in condition-specific communities
- Users who already keep symptom diaries

### 10-star experience

A normal experience:

- Device shows a new metric.

A better experience:

- User sees a signal related to symptoms.

A 10-star experience:

- User finally has language for a body experience they struggled to explain.
- The product helps them connect symptoms, context, and body signal.
- The user feels seen and less alone.

### Brian Chesky Lens judgment

For new-metric wearables, emotional value may come from explanation, not just measurement.

Useful judgment:

> 新指标真正打动早期用户的，不是数据本身，而是“终于有人解释了我的身体处境”。

---

## 10. How to Combine with Wang Product Philosophy

Brian Chesky Lens helps design early love and high-touch learning.

But final judgment must return to Wang Product Philosophy.

After using this lens, still ask:

- Is the opportunity real?
- Is the first target user precise?
- Is the demand strong?
- Is the value anchor hard enough?
- Does the price anchor make sense?
- Can the product survive hardware reality?
- Can the business model work after handcrafted service ends?
- What can be scaled, and what should remain personal?

Useful judgment:

> Brian Chesky Lens 可以帮我们找到早期热爱；王师傅主脑要继续判断这份热爱能不能转化成购买、留存、口碑和长期生意。

---

## 11. Final Brian Chesky Lens Rule

When uncertain, use this rule:

Do not ask how to scale first.

Ask who will love it first.

Then design the highest-touch, most emotionally clear experience for that small group.

Only after real love appears should the team decide what to automate, productize, and scale.
